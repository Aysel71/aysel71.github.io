About

Generative models that know what you want

I'm a research scientist working in a video generation team, focused on personalization — building generative models that can synthesize video and images conditioned on a specific person's identity, appearance, style, or preferences.

Personalization in generative models is harder than it sounds. The core challenge is making a model adapt to an individual — from a handful of reference images or signals — without losing the quality, diversity, and controllability of the underlying generation. My work explores how identity and preference information can be encoded, injected, and preserved across temporal sequences in video generation.

My background spans mathematical economics at HSE and LSE (First Class Honours) and deep learning at Skoltech. Actively applying to PhD programs in ML and computer vision. English (IELTS 8.0), Russian (native), Turkish (fluent).

Statement of Purpose

Why a PhD

My path into research was not linear — it passed through economics, mathematical finance, and language models before converging, with growing conviction, on the question that now defines my work: how do we build generative models that truly understand what a person wants, and can synthesize video and images that reflect that understanding across time?

During my undergraduate years at HSE and the London School of Economics, I was trained to think rigorously about uncertainty and sequential decision-making. My thesis on reinforcement learning for optimal execution gave me a deep appreciation for the challenge of temporal reasoning under constraints — an intuition that carried directly into my current work on video generation, where the model must maintain coherence and intent across an entire sequence.

Working in a video generation team, I have focused on personalization — the problem of making generative models adapt to a specific person's identity, appearance, and style, from just a few reference signals. This sounds tractable but runs into fundamental difficulties: how do you encode identity in a way that transfers across frames, preserves temporal coherence, and doesn't collapse the model's generative diversity? Our work on this resulted in a publication at CVPR 2026.

A PhD would give me the depth and intellectual freedom to pursue the open questions I care about most: how personalization can be made robust and controllable at scale, how identity information should be represented across temporal sequences, and how we build generative systems that are genuinely responsive to the individual — not just conditioned on a concept, but adapted to a person. I am looking for an advisor who shares this interest in the foundations of personalized generative modeling — and who sees that the hardest problems here are still very much open.
